Abstract
Fine-grained energy attribution — determining how much energy individual software
entities consume at runtime — is a prerequisite for any software-level approach to managing or
optimising the energy consumption of modern computing infrastructure. This thesis examines
the current state of that capability in Linux systems.
The work surveys the hardware measurement interfaces and dynamic processor mechanisms that govern what energy data is available and how it behaves, analyses the structural
challenges that make attributing that data to individual processes and containers difficult,
and critically evaluates the tools and approaches the research community has developed in
response. The review organises existing work by attribution goal and methodological family,
distinguishing model-based from formula-based approaches and tracing the progress each has
made.
The analysis shows that meaningful progress has been made: model-based approaches
have advanced toward fully adaptive, self-calibrating systems that require no offline calibration, while formula-based approaches have progressively extended their hardware coverage to
jointly address dynamic frequency scaling, simultaneous multithreading, and Non-Uniform
Memory Access (NUMA) topology. Yet fundamental limitations persist across both families.
No existing tool achieves real-time coverage of all relevant hardware mechanisms without
excessive overhead. Idle and residual energy are not consistently separated from active attribution. And the proportional distribution mechanism that underlies virtually all reviewed
approaches cannot correctly isolate energy changes at the granularity at which they originate
— a structural flaw no amount of calibration or more expressive formulas can resolve. These
three dimensions together define the open problem the existing literature has not closed.
Keywords— Fine-grained energy attribution, power measurement, Linux, Running Average
Power Limit (RAPL), software-defined power meters, Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA), Simultaneous Multithreading (SMT)

Résumé
L’attribution énergétique fine — déterminer la quantité d’énergie consommée par chaque entité
logicielle individuelle à l’exécution — est un prérequis pour toute approche logicielle visant à gérer
ou optimiser la consommation énergétique des infrastructures informatiques modernes. Cette thèse
examine l’état actuel de cette capacité dans les systèmes Linux.
Le travail passe en revue les interfaces de mesure matérielles et les mécanismes dynamiques
des processeurs qui déterminent quelles données énergétiques sont disponibles et comment elles
se comportent, analyse les défis structurels qui rendent difficile l’attribution de ces données à des
processus et conteneurs individuels, et évalue de manière critique les outils et approches développés
en réponse par la communauté de recherche. La revue organise les travaux existants selon l’objectif
d’attribution et la famille méthodologique, en distinguant les approches basées sur des modèles de
celles basées sur des formules, et en retraçant les progrès accomplis par chacune.
L’analyse montre que des progrès significatifs ont été réalisés : les approches basées sur des
modèles ont évolué vers des systèmes entièrement adaptatifs et auto-calibrés ne nécessitant aucune
calibration hors ligne, tandis que les approches basées sur des formules ont progressivement étendu
leur couverture matérielle pour traiter conjointement la mise à l’échelle dynamique de fréquence, le
multithreading simultané et la topologie Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA). Cependant, des
limitations fondamentales persistent dans les deux familles. Aucun outil existant ne réalise une couverture en temps réel de tous les mécanismes matériels pertinents sans surcharge excessive. L’énergie
inactive et résiduelle n’est pas systématiquement séparée de l’attribution active. Et le mécanisme
de distribution proportionnelle qui sous-tend pratiquement toutes les approches examinées ne peut
pas isoler correctement les variations énergétiques à la granularité à laquelle elles se produisent —
un défaut structurel qu’aucune calibration ni formule plus expressive ne peut résoudre. Ces trois
dimensions définissent ensemble le problème ouvert que la littérature existante n’a pas résolu.
Keywords— Attribution énergétique fine, mesure de puissance, Linux, Running Average Power
Limit (RAPL), wattmètres logiciels, Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA), Simultaneous Multithreading (SMT)

INTRODUCTION

Context
Accurate, real-time visibility into the energy consumed by individual software entities — processes, containers, virtual machines — is a prerequisite for a broad class of approaches to managing
and optimising the energy footprint of modern computing infrastructure. Energy-aware scheduling, workload placement, and consumption-based accountability all depend on being able to answer
the same basic question at runtime: how much energy is this software entity drawing right now?
Without that capability, the system cannot be observed at the granularity at which it can be acted
upon.
This need has sharpened considerably in recent years. Data centre energy consumption is growing rapidly under the pressure of Artificial Intelligence (AI) workloads, with electricity demand
projected to approach 945 TWh globally by 2030. Efficiency improvements at the hardware and
infrastructure level, while necessary, are insufficient on their own: measuring and attributing energy
consumption accurately, down to the workload and process level, is a prerequisite for directing optimisation efforts where they will have genuine impact. Fine-grained energy attribution is no longer
solely an operational concern for cost management — it is an environmental and infrastructural
necessity.

Problem Statement
Despite its importance, fine-grained energy attribution at the process and container level remains
an unsolved problem. Hardware energy interfaces — most notably Intel and Advanced Micro Devices
(AMD)’s Running Average Power Limit (RAPL) — report aggregate consumption at the Central
Processing Unit (CPU) package and Dynamic Random Access Memory (DRAM) domain level, not
at the granularity of individual software entities. Bridging this gap requires constructing an indirect
model: observe how each software entity uses hardware resources, and use those observations as a
basis for partitioning the measured total. This makes attribution inherently a modelling problem
rather than a measurement problem.
The challenge is compounded by the dynamic behaviour of modern processors: continuous percore frequency and voltage adjustments, idle power state transitions, simultaneous multithreading,
and Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA) topology all cause the relationship between observable
software activity and actual energy consumption to be non-linear, time-varying, and hardwarespecific. Existing tools and approaches address these complications to varying degrees, but no
current solution jointly achieves real-time coverage of all relevant hardware mechanisms, consistent
treatment of idle and residual energy, and overhead compatible with production deployment.

11

Research Goals
This thesis examines whether fine-grained CPU and DRAM energy attribution currently exists
as a practical capability for Linux systems. Specifically, it aims to:
• Establish the technical foundations governing what energy data is available and at what
granularity on modern hardware.
• Analyse the structural challenges that make attributing that data to individual software
entities difficult.
• Survey and critically evaluate the tools and approaches the research community has developed
in response, organised by attribution goal and methodological family.
• Synthesise the findings into a comparative analysis that identifies where meaningful progress
has been made and where fundamental limitations persist.
The scope is intentionally bounded to CPU and DRAM attribution at the process and container
level, as these are the primary targets in the existing literature and the level at which attribution

CHAPTER 1
ENERGY CONSUMPTION AND SUSTAINABILITY

Sustainability has been a growing concern in the computing industry for several decades, driven
initially by operational economics and more recently by environmental imperatives.

1.1

Total Cost of Ownership and the Energy Problem

The earliest sustained interest in energy efficiency in data centres was grounded not in environmental concern but in cost. Structured Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) modelling for data centres
identified five main cost components:
• Infrastructure — physical facility construction and maintenance
• Server acquisition — hardware procurement and refresh cycles
• Networking — interconnect infrastructure
• Power — energy consumed by IT equipment and the facility systems supporting it
• Maintenance — ongoing operational labour and support costs
Of these, power consistently emerged as a primary target for optimisation, not because it was
necessarily the largest component in absolute terms, but because it was the most sensitive to
engineering decisions [1]. Energy cost in a data centre has two layers: the electricity consumed by
servers themselves, and the overhead consumed by the cooling, lighting, and power distribution
infrastructure that supports them. Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE), introduced in 2007 and
endorsed by The Green Grid as a global standard, was developed precisely to make this distinction
legible — it measures the ratio of total facility energy to IT equipment energy, giving operators a
single number that captures how efficiently power reaches useful work [2]. Operating cost is linearly
dependent on PUE, making it the central lever through which infrastructure and cooling choices
translate directly into financial outcomes [1].
This cost framing motivated two parallel research directions: optimising the energy efficiency
of server hardware itself, and improving the energy efficiency of data centre infrastructure. On
the hardware side, an influential example is the research by Barroso and Hölzle, who established
that servers spend most of their time at 10–50% of peak utilisation, yet consume close to half
their peak power even while nearly idle, and proposed Energy-Proportional Computing (EPC) —
hardware that consumes energy in direct proportion to the work it performs — as the foundational
design goal [3]. On the infrastructure side, PUE reduction became a major engineering direction,
pursued through approaches such as direct liquid cooling, alternative power delivery architectures,
and evaporative cooling systems [1].

15

As hardware and infrastructure efficiency improved, and as virtualisation became the dominant deployment model, a third direction emerged: attributing energy consumption to the software
workloads running on servers. If a physical server runs dozens of virtual machines or containers simultaneously, knowing its aggregate power draw is insufficient for cost allocation, workload
scheduling, or capacity planning. This motivated a body of work on per-Virtual Machine (VM) and
per-container power modelling and metering that extended the TCO optimisation concern down to
the software layer [4, 5, 6, 7].

1.2

The AI Era: New Patterns, New Challenges

The emergence of Artificial Intelligence (AI) as a dominant data centre workload has introduced
a qualitatively different set of pressures on top of the existing cost and efficiency concerns. AI
workloads require high-performance accelerated servers whose power density significantly exceeds
that of conventional server deployments, and they operate at a scale and growth rate that existing
infrastructure was not designed to absorb.
Some of these challenges are already materialising:
• The three largest hyperscalers reported a 54% increase in data centre capital expenditure
between 2022 and 2023, rising from $78 billion to $120 billion [8], reflecting the pace at which
physical infrastructure is being built out to meet AI demand.
• A number of power grids have already halted or slowed new data centre connections to
preserve grid reliability, with documented cases including Dominion Energy in Virginia [9]
and EirGrid in Ireland [10], with the shortfall in the most stressed grids severe enough that
even under the most permissive scenarios available capacity cannot meet projected demand
[8].
• The inability of grids to meet demand has led to climate-unfriendly measures, including
delaying the retirement of fossil-based generation plants [8].
Others are projected based on current trajectories:
• The International Energy Agency (IEA) projects data centre electricity demand reaching approximately 945 TWh by 2030, representing just under 3% of global electricity consumption,
with electricity consumption in accelerated servers growing at roughly 30% per year compared
to 9% for conventional servers [11].
• Data centre Carbon Dioxide (CO2) emissions are projected to reach approximately 1% of
global emissions by 2030, making data centres one of the few sectors where emissions are
expected to grow rather than decline as others decarbonise [11].
• AI training and inference carry substantial and growing carbon footprints — Meta’s Llama370B carries more than six times the training carbon footprint of its predecessor, and inference
optimisation can further multiply total emissions by two to three times [12, 13].
A structural dimension compounds all of these challenges. As hardware becomes more efficient,
the lower cost of computation encourages higher usage, potentially offsetting efficiency gains through
increased demand — the economic phenomenon known as Jevons’s paradox [12]. This means that
efficiency improvements at the hardware or infrastructure level, while necessary, are not sufficient
on their own: measuring and attributing energy consumption accurately, down to the workload and
process level, becomes a prerequisite for directing optimisation efforts where they will have genuine
impact and for verifying that those efforts are not simply absorbed by demand growth.
The trajectory described here — from TCO-driven hardware optimisation, through infrastructure efficiency, to software-level attribution, and now to the intensified pressures of the AI era —

establishes the context in which the research examined in this work operates. The gradual shift
of focus toward software-level energy visibility was already underway before AI reshaped the landscape; the new pressures have accelerated that shift and raised its stakes considerably. Fine-grained
energy attribution is no longer solely an operational concern for data centre cost management — it
is an environmental and infrastructural necessity. Realising it, however, first requires understanding
how energy information can be obtained from the hardware in the first place — the subject of the
following section.

Energy and power are related but distinct physical quantities. Power is the instantaneous rate
at which work is performed, while energy is the total work done over a period of time. They are

2.2

External Power Measurement

External power measurement refers to instruments placed outside the compute node itself,
typically between the wall socket and the power supply, or integrated into the data centre power
delivery infrastructure. They share a common characteristic: they observe the node as a whole and
report aggregate consumption without any visibility into individual internal components.

2.2.1

Dedicated External Watt Meters

Dedicated external power meters, such as the ZES ZIMMER LMG450 or the ZES LMG670,
offer the highest accuracy of any measurement category.
Despite this precision, they are constrained to sampling intervals of 50 ms at best, and other
widely used instruments such as the Watts Up Pro, Cray PMDB, and Yokogawa WT230 operate at
intervals of 100 ms or slower [15]. All external meters share a further fundamental limitation: they

2.2.2

Power Distribution Units and IPMI

At the infrastructure level, Power Distribution Unit (PDU) with measurement capability and

data centres, primarily because they require no additional hardware and scale without per-machine
instrumentation.
Both PDUs and IPMI-based interfaces typically report at intervals of around 1 second [17], and
like dedicated external meters, they operate at the node level with no visibility into individual components — making them suitable for coarse energy accounting but not for fine-grained attribution.

2.2.3

Taken together, external measurement approaches share two fundamental limitations that restrict their applicability to process-level or component-level energy attribution:

• Low temporal resolution. Even the most precise dedicated meters are limited to roughly
50 ms intervals, and infrastructure-level interfaces such as IPMI operate at 1 Hz or slower

• No component-level visibility. Every reading reflects the entire node; there is no way

2.3

On-Chip Power Metering

On-chip power interfaces are software-accessible mechanisms built directly into modern processors and GPUs. Unlike external instruments, they do not require additional hardware, introduce
negligible overhead, and expose consumption at the component level. Their trade-offs differ by
vendor and generation, and understanding those differences is necessary for interpreting the values
they produce.

2.3.1

Running Average Power Limit

Running Average Power Limit (RAPL) is a hardware-level energy monitoring interface that
exposes per-domain energy counters accumulating continuously from processor boot, requiring no
initialisation. It carries negligible measurement overhead — tests on standalone systems show
a maximum overhead of approximately 1.2% even at sampling frequencies up to 1.1 kHz [19].
Both Intel and Advanced Micro Devices (AMD) expose RAPL interfaces on modern processors,
though their implementations differ in domain coverage, measurement approach, and accuracy
characteristics, as detailed in the subsections below.

2.3.1.1 RAPL Domains
RAPL organises energy reporting around the concept of power domains: hardware-scoped units
that each report cumulative energy consumption for a specific component or sub-part of the processor. In multi-socket systems, each domain is scoped per socket. The set of available domains varies
by processor architecture and is illustrated in Figure 2.4.
The domains are as follows [19]:
• Package (CPU socket) (PKG) — Reports total energy consumption of the entire socket,
including all cores, integrated graphics, and uncore components such as last-level caches and
the memory controller. This is the only universally supported domain across all RAPLcapable architectures.

• Power Plane 0 (PP0) — Reports the combined energy of all processor cores on the socket.
It is a subset of PKG, meaning its value is already included in the PKG total.
• Power Plane 1 (PP1) — Reports energy consumed by the processor’s integrated GPU.
Available on desktop models only and also a subset of PKG.
• Dynamic Random Access Memory (DRAM) — Reports energy for the RAM directly
attached to the integrated memory controller of a given socket. In multi-socket systems, each

• PSys (Platform) — Introduced with Skylake, this domain monitors energy for the entire
System-on-Chip (SoC), including components attributed to neither the CPU nor the GPU

Access Memory (eDRAM). Its exact scope is manufacturer-dependent, and on tested platforms it was found to match readings from an external wattmeter [20].

2.3.1.2 Accessing RAPL
RAPL counters can be accessed through four mechanisms [20]:
• Model-Specific Register (MSR) — Direct register access requiring vendor- and modelspecific knowledge of register offsets and unit conversions. Demands high privileges and is
the most fragile approach.
• Linux powercap sysfs interface — Exposes measurements as readable text files, requires

• perf-events via Extended Berkeley Packet Filter (eBPF) — Offers no practical performance
advantage over standard perf-events for straightforward sampling, despite significantly higher
implementation complexity [20]. However, it becomes highly relevant when energy readings
need to be coupled with kernel-level scheduling events such as context switches, which is
important for tools aiming at finer attribution granularity.

2.3.1.3 Intel RAPL
Intel introduced RAPL with the Sandy Bridge microarchitecture in 2011 [18]. Its accuracy
has improved substantially across processor generations, with the Haswell generation introducing
on-chip voltage regulators that enabled actual physical power measurement rather than modelbased estimation, bringing PKG readings to a correlation coefficient of approximately 0.99 against
wall-socket power [19]. This combination of component-level granularity, sub-millisecond update
rates, and close correlation with true power draw is what makes RAPL the dominant measurement
interface in the energy attribution literature. Skylake further improved the temporal resolution of
the PP0 domain to 50–70 µs updates [19].

2.3.1.4 AMD RAPL
AMD adopted RAPL with the introduction of the Zen architecture, reading out through different MSRs than Intel but following a broadly similar domain structure [21]. The accuracy of AMD’s
monitoring mechanisms directly influences processor behaviour, since they underpin frequency selection and power capping decisions [21].
AMD’s RAPL implementation relies on a model-based approach, similar to Intel’s original Sandy
Bridge implementation rather than the measurement-based approach Intel introduced with Haswell
[21]. In terms of domains, AMD exposes only the package and core domains; notably, AMD’s
core domain provides per-core spatial resolution, whereas Intel’s PP0 reports at the per-package
level [21]. The update rate is 1 ms [21]. AMD’s RAPL has two important limitations for energy
attribution:
• No DRAM domain. DRAM energy consumption is not fully reflected in the package
domain, meaning total system energy cannot be accurately reconstructed from RAPL readings
alone on AMD hardware [21].
• Data-dependent blindness. The model fails to capture variations in power consumption
caused by the nature of the processed data for data processing workloads: differences measurable externally do not appear in RAPL readings, which affects attribution accuracy [21].

22

2.3.1.5 AMD Application Power Management (Legacy)
Prior to Zen, AMD processors exposed power data through the Application Power Management
(APM) interface, which AMD replaced with RAPL starting with the Zen architecture [21]. Unlike
RAPL’s accumulating energy counters, APM reported the average power consumption over the most
recent capture window, with a default update rate of approximately 10 ms [17]. This design sidesteps
the timestamp ambiguity inherent in RAPL’s accumulator approach, though APM suffered from
significant inaccuracies depending on system configuration particularly related to the CPU [17].
APM is relevant only for pre-Zen AMD hardware and should not be expected on modern systems.

2.3.2

GPU On-Chip Interfaces

Modern GPUs expose power consumption through vendor-specific software interfaces. NVIDIA
GPUs provide power readings via the NVIDIA Management Library (NVML), a C-based Application Programming Interface (API) bundled with the GPU driver, while AMD GPUs expose
equivalent data through ROCm System Management Interface (SMI). Both eliminate the need for
external instrumentation and provide component-level visibility comparable to CPU-side RAPL
interfaces. GPU attribution is outside the scope of this thesis; these interfaces are noted here for
completeness, but the remainder of this work focuses exclusively on CPU and DRAM.

CHAPTER 3
ENERGY-ORIENTED HARDWARE MECHANISMS

This chapter explores the physical and logic-level processor and memory mechanisms that govern
power usage. We look at static vs. dynamic power, frequency scaling, idle power management,
simultaneous multithreading, and Non-Uniform Memory Access architecture.

3.1

Static and Dynamic Power

At a general level, the power consumed by a hardware resource can be decomposed into two
components. Static power is the power drawn at rest — the baseline consumption of a resource
regardless of whether it is doing any useful work. Dynamic power is the additional power resulting
from activity on that resource: it arises only when the resource is actively processing or transferring
data, and scales with the intensity of that activity. The exact mechanisms through which these two
components manifest depend on the type of resource. In the context of processors, static power
arises from leakage currents that flow through transistors regardless of any switching activity, while
dynamic power is the power consumed by actual computation and scales with supply voltage and
clock frequency [22].
These two components have fundamentally different implications for power management. Static
power represents an unavoidable baseline that can only be eliminated by physically cutting power to
the resource. Dynamic power is workload-dependent and can be reduced by lowering activity or, in
the case of processors, by lowering the operating voltage and frequency. The hardware mechanisms
described in the following subsections target one or both of these quantities.

3.2

Frequency and Voltage Scaling

Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling (DVFS) is the hardware mechanism that allows a
processor to adjust its operating frequency and supply voltage at runtime. DVFS operates at the
individual core level: each core independently manages its own frequency and voltage. Central
Processing Unit (CPU) threads running on the same core share its frequency and voltage — there
is no per-thread frequency setting [22].
The reason frequency and voltage are adjusted together lies in how transistor circuits operate.
Reducing voltage slows down the charging and discharging of capacitors within the circuit, which
in turn limits how fast it can switch reliably — necessitating a corresponding reduction in clock
frequency to match. Since dynamic power scales quadratically with voltage, this reduction alone
yields large energy savings; the accompanying frequency reduction amplifies them further, as the
processor completes fewer operations per unit of time. Raising frequency has the opposite effect:

24

the circuit must switch faster, requiring a higher voltage to do so reliably, resulting in substantially
greater power draw [22].
DVFS is used to dynamically adapt power consumption to workload demands in both directions.
When a core has no computational work to do — for example, while waiting on a memory access or
an I/O operation — its frequency and voltage can be reduced to save energy, since performance is
not the bottleneck at that moment. When demand rises again, frequency and voltage are raised to
restore throughput. Whether doing so is beneficial, however, depends on the nature of the workload:
for memory-bound applications, raising CPU frequency can increase energy consumption without
any meaningful performance gain, because execution time is dominated by memory access latency
rather than computation speed [23].
In practice, DVFS is not exposed as a continuous dial. Instead, processors define a discrete set
of validated frequency-voltage pairs called Performance states (P-states), each indexed from zero
upward and guaranteed to operate correctly. A lower index corresponds to higher frequency, higher
voltage, and greater throughput at the cost of greater power draw; a higher index corresponds to
lower frequency and lower voltage. Up to 16 P-states may be defined for a given processor [22]. The
Advanced Configuration and Power Interface (ACPI) specification [24] standardises this abstraction
and provides the interface through which the operating system manages processor frequency and
power states [23]. Historically, P-state selection was the responsibility of the operating system,
but modern processors have largely delegated this to hardware through Hardware-controlled Performance States, though software override and full disabling of the feature remain possible [22].
Vendor implementations may carry distinct names in their documentation: Intel’s, for example, is
documented as Enhanced Intel SpeedStep Technology.

3.3

Dynamic Performance Boosting

P-states define the normal operating frequencies and voltages of a processor core, with P0
representing the highest performance state. Under favourable thermal and power conditions, the
processor can opportunistically boost individual core frequencies up to the ceiling defined by P0
to maximise performance — a mechanism documented as Intel Turbo Boost Technology on Intel processors and Precision Boost on Advanced Micro Devices (AMD) processors. This elevated
frequency requires a correspondingly higher voltage, raising power draw for the duration of the
boost, with the additional throughput coming from more instructions being processed per unit of
time. The processor determines how much of a boost is sustainable by continuously monitoring
activity and idle states to track available thermal and energy headroom and estimate current power
consumption. Like P-states, boosting operates at the core level: individual cores can be boosted
independently, and hardware threads on a boosted core share its elevated frequency. Cores that are
not being boosted are correspondingly throttled or held at lower P-states to keep total chip power
dissipation within the chip’s Thermal Design Power (TDP) limit [23, 22].

3.4

Idle Power Management

When a core has no work to do, the processor reduces power by entering an idle state. The
hardware achieves this through two underlying primitives.

3.4.1

Clock Gating

Clock gating cuts the clock signal to parts of the circuit that are inactive, eliminating dynamic
power for those blocks. It operates at low cost and with low transition latency, making it well-suited
to short or intermittent idle periods [22].

25

3.4.2

Power Gating

Power gating goes further, physically cutting the power supply to entire circuit blocks and
eliminating both static and dynamic power for those blocks. It achieves greater energy savings than
clock gating, but incurs a larger penalty when entering and exiting the gated state, making it more
appropriate for sustained periods of inactivity [22].

3.4.3

C-states

Building on these two primitives, the ACPI specification defines processor idle states — referred
to as C-states — as the abstraction through which the operating system and hardware coordinate
idle power management. C-states are numbered from zero upward: C0 is the active state in which
a core is executing instructions, and higher numbers correspond to progressively deeper levels of
inactivity. Entering a deeper C-state gates more hardware units, trading greater energy savings for
longer wake-up latency. Hardware vendors may define additional substates beyond those mandated
by ACPI, and naming conventions vary across vendors [23].
C-states are managed at two levels. Core C-states control per-core unit gating and are selected
by the operating system. Although individual hardware threads can each request a desired idle
level, a C-state is applied to the whole core rather than to individual threads. A core can therefore
only enter a given C-state when all of its hardware threads have requested at least that level of
idleness — a single active thread keeps the entire core in a shallower state. Package C-states, which
operate at the level of the entire processor package, extend this logic to shared chip resources: the
hardware will only engage deeper package-level gating when all cores have reached a sufficiently deep
Core C-state, meaning a single active thread anywhere on the chip can prevent the entire package
from entering deep idle states [22]. Unlike P-states, which the crfullos selects per core, Package
C-states are managed autonomously by the hardware; the Operating System (OS) influences them
only indirectly through its Core C-state selections.

3.5

Simultaneous Multithreading

A hardware thread is a complete set of per-thread architectural state — registers, program
counter, and execution context — maintained directly in hardware within a core. Having this state
replicated in hardware allows a core to track multiple execution contexts simultaneously without
software intervention. This is what CPU specifications refer to when listing thread count alongside
core count: a processor advertised as ”4 cores, 8 threads” has 4 physical cores, each capable of
maintaining 2 hardware thread contexts. How those contexts are used — whether the core switches
between them over time or executes them concurrently — is what distinguishes the two forms of
hardware multithreading.
The simpler form of multithreading, Temporal Multithreading (TMT), allows a core to host
multiple hardware threads but issues instructions from only one of them per cycle. Simultaneous
Multithreading (SMT) — documented by Intel as Hyper-Threading Technology — goes further:
it issues instructions from multiple hardware threads within the same cycle, filling execution units
that would otherwise sit idle. The primary motivation is improved CPU utilisation: when one
thread stalls waiting on a memory access, instructions from another thread can occupy the available
resources rather than leaving them unused [25]. From the operating system’s perspective, this makes
a processor with N physical cores appear as 2N logical cores [23]. Because both hardware threads
run on the same physical core, they share its frequency, voltage, and execution resources — SMT
provides no per-thread frequency setting. The throughput benefit holds only when threads are
not competing too heavily for shared resources such as caches; heavy contention can negate the
advantage entirely [25].

26

While SMT is a performance mechanism rather than an energy management one, it has direct
implications for energy consumption through two effects. First, execution units that would otherwise
be clock-gated during idle periods are kept active by instructions from the second thread, reducing or
eliminating the savings that clock gating would otherwise provide. Second, some internal buffers and
queues must be enlarged to hold in-flight instructions from two threads simultaneously, increasing
static power consumption independent of utilisation [26]. Together, these effects mean that enabling
SMT consistently increases power draw, making it effectively a trade-off between performance and
energy consumption. As a concrete illustration, concurrent execution under SMT has been shown to
increase total energy usage by approximately 15% compared to single-threaded execution, while still
consuming less energy than distributing the same work across two separate physical cores [27]. In
cases where the performance gain is modest, enabling SMT can increase total energy consumption
by up to 21% compared to running with it disabled [23].

3.6

Non-Uniform Memory Access

The preceding mechanisms govern energy consumption on the processor side. Memory introduces its own set of energy characteristics, shaped both by how Dynamic Random Access Memory
(DRAM) behaves as a component and by how memory is physically organised across the system.

3.6.1

Architecture and Performance Characteristics

Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA) is a memory architecture in which the latency and available bandwidth of a memory access depend on the physical location of the target memory relative to
the requesting processor. In systems where memory is distributed across multiple sockets, accesses
to different memory regions traverse paths of different lengths and may cross shared interconnects,
resulting in measurably different performance characteristics [28].

Figure 3.1: Two-NUMA-node system diagram
In a typical multi-socket server, each CPU socket has its own directly attached DRAM, and the
system is partitioned into NUMA nodes accordingly — each node consisting of a processor socket
and the memory physically connected to it, as illustrated in Figure 3.1. Accessing memory within
the local node is handled by the local memory controller without crossing any shared interconnect,
and is therefore faster. Accessing memory on a remote node requires traversing the inter-socket

27

interconnect, introducing additional latency and subjecting that link — and the remote memory
controller — to contention from concurrent accesses, resulting in measurable degradation in both
latency and throughput [28].
NUMA placement refers to the policy that governs which NUMA node a given memory allocation is assigned to. Since all memory within a node presents the same access characteristics to
a local processor, placing allocations on the node closest to the processor that will use them minimises latency and avoids interconnect contention. Poor placement forces frequent remote accesses,
increasing latency and reducing the effective memory bandwidth available to the workload [28].

3.6.2

Energy Characteristics of Memory in Multi-Socket Systems

DRAM power consumption has two components. The first is a relatively stable background
component arising from operations that occur independently of access activity — most notably the
periodic refresh cycles required to preserve stored data. At moderate access rates, this background
component dominates and overall power consumption remains broadly stable, regardless of how
much of the installed memory capacity is in use. Under heavy memory activity, as in memoryintensive workloads, the dynamic component driven by actual read and write operations becomes
significant and raises overall consumption accordingly [29].
The NUMA topology introduces a second energy dimension. Remote memory accesses incur not
only a performance penalty but also an energy penalty: traversing the inter-socket interconnect and
engaging a remote memory controller consumes more energy than a local access handled entirely
within the same node [30, 31]. NUMA placement therefore has a direct effect on energy efficiency
— local-node allocation minimises both latency and energy cost per access, while poor placement
increases both.
The mechanisms covered in this section collectively shape the energy profile of a running system.
On the processor side, DVFS and P-states provide the primary levers for active power management,
continuously adjusting per-core voltage and frequency in response to workload demand, while Cstates allow individual cores — and the processor package as a whole — to shed power during
idle periods. Dynamic performance boosting introduces a third operating regime above the normal baseline, trading higher instantaneous power for greater throughput when thermal conditions
permit. Simultaneous Multithreading, while primarily a performance feature, reshapes the energy
picture by keeping execution units active that would otherwise be clock-gated, consistently raising
power draw independent of any explicit power management setting. On the memory side, NUMA
topology introduces access-cost asymmetry that affects not only latency and bandwidth but also
energy: remote memory accesses consume more energy than local ones, and the fixed power draw
of DRAM means that how bandwidth is used — rather than how much memory is occupied —
determines memory energy efficiency. With both the measurement interfaces and the hardware
mechanisms that shape their readings now established, the following section examines what makes
attributing those readings to individual software entities structurally difficult.

CHAPTER 4
CHALLENGES IN SOFTWARE ENERGY ATTRIBUTION

This chapter details the fundamental limits and practical complexities of software-level energy
attribution, concluding with a review of the operating system tools and kernel interfaces used to
observe application behaviour.

4.1

The Fundamental Attribution Gap

The starting point for every challenge in this space is a basic asymmetry: hardware energy
meters report aggregate consumption at the component level, while the question being asked —
how much did this process consume? — is at the software entity level. As established in Chapter 2,
Running Average Power Limit (RAPL) exposes readings per Central Processing Unit (CPU) package
and per Dynamic Random Access Memory (DRAM) domain [27]. It does not report per-core, perthread, or per-process consumption. On a system running dozens of concurrent processes, RAPL
answers ”how much did this socket consume?” but not ”by whom?” [32].
Bridging this gap requires constructing an indirect model: observe how each software entity
uses hardware resources, and use those observations as a basis for partitioning the measured total.
This makes attribution inherently a modelling problem rather than a measurement problem — and
it means that every attribution result carries the assumptions and limitations of the model used to
produce it [27]. There is no ground truth at the per-process level against which a model can be
directly validated; the only available check is whether the per-entity attributions sum back to the
measured component total [33, 32]. This validation by summation confirms aggregate consistency
but cannot verify that energy has been correctly distributed among individual entities.

4.2

Hardware Complexity and Variability

The hardware is not a stable, predictable environment. Processors continuously adjust their
operating frequency and voltage, enter and exit idle power states, share execution resources across
threads, and impose topology-dependent costs on memory accesses — and each of these behaviours
changes the relationship between observable software activity and actual energy consumption in
ways that a naive attribution model will not capture.
A first dimension of this complexity is heterogeneity. Hardware varies enormously across vendors, generations, and configurations, and energy behaviour is deeply coupled to those specifics. A
model that accurately captures energy consumption on one platform carries no guarantee of correctness on another — not even across nominally identical devices, where measurable variation in power
consumption has been observed between units of the same hardware model [34, 27]. This makes

29

generalisation structurally difficult: any attribution approach is, to some degree, platform-specific
[35].
A second dimension is availability. The interfaces and counters that attribution tools depend on
— such as hardware performance counters or RAPL domains — are not uniformly present across
hardware and kernel versions. What is exposed on one architecture may be absent or differently
scoped on another, meaning that a model designed around a particular set of observables may have
no equivalent data source on a different platform [27]. Where counters are available, hardware constraints on how many can be active simultaneously introduce a further limitation: when more events
are requested than physical registers allow, the kernel must multiplex them over time, introducing
sampling error into the very metrics the model relies on [32].
A third dimension is runtime variability. At any given moment, the state of the system can differ
significantly from one measurement interval to the next. Dynamic power management mechanisms
continuously adjust operating conditions at the core level — varying frequency, voltage, and idle
depth — in response to workload demand, making the energy cost of the same observable activity
non-constant over time. Parallel execution introduces additional variability: two threads running
simultaneously on the same core produce a different energy profile than the same two threads
running in sequence, independent of any explicit power management activity. The result is that
even under controlled conditions, the relationship between a software entity’s observable resource
usage and its actual energy contribution is unstable across time and across runs [35].

4.3

Attribution Ambiguity

Given a hardware energy reading, the problem of partitioning it among concurrent software
entities is not merely a matter of finding the right proxy metric — it is structurally ambiguous in
several ways that no choice of proxy fully resolves.
A first source of ambiguity is workload diversity. Workloads differ significantly in how they
interact with hardware — in their instruction mix, memory access patterns, I/O behaviour, and
degree of parallelism — and the energy interfaces available do not always offer the granularity
needed to capture these differences. The result is that the same observable metric can correspond
to very different actual energy expenditures depending on what the workload is doing, making any
single attribution proxy unreliable across a heterogeneous mix of software [32].
A second source is shared resource ownership. Hardware resources are used concurrently by
multiple software entities, and the energy they consume has no clear owner. More fundamentally,
a software entity’s energy footprint is not an intrinsic property — it is shaped by the behaviour of
everything else running alongside it, making attribution inherently a joint rather than independent
accounting problem [32]. Some of these dependencies are indirect, such as cache contention causing
one workload to increase another’s memory energy cost; others are structural, such as shared address
spaces with no unambiguous ownership, or memory operations that are decoupled from the thread
that initiated them due to cache write-back for example [33, 27].
A third source is the interaction between co-running software entities. The energy behaviour
of a system running software in isolation differs substantially from one running the same software
alongside competing workloads. In multi-tenant environments in particular, collocated workloads
influence the host’s total energy reading, and the side-effects of that contention — on frequency,
on resource availability, on memory locality — are difficult to disentangle from each entity’s own
contribution [33].
Finally, validation itself is structurally limited. The only practical check available to an attribution model is that per-entity attributions sum back to the measured component total — but
satisfying this constraint says nothing about whether energy has been correctly distributed among
individual entities. This aggregate consistency can mask significant per-process inaccuracies, a
problem that has been demonstrated concretely in practice [36].

30

4.4

Practical and Systemic Constraints

Beyond the hardware and attribution challenges above, a set of practical constraints shapes
what any real attribution tool can actually observe and how faithfully it can report.
A first constraint is the system itself. The operating system and its runtime activity — scheduling, background processes, managed runtime overhead — are not passive observers; they consume
energy and continuously alter the conditions under which software runs. This activity cannot always
be cleanly separated from the software under test, and the system’s own management decisions,
such as migrating a thread between cores with different energy profiles, introduce variability into
the very signal the tool is trying to capture [34, 35].
A second constraint is the tool’s own footprint. Any attribution tool consumes resources in
the act of observing, and beyond a certain point that overhead begins to perturb the energy measurements it is trying to produce [35, 27]. For polling-based approaches, this manifests as a direct
tension between sampling frequency and measurement fidelity. Supporting virtualised environments
adds a further layer of complexity: energy-related hardware state is generally not propagated into
guest environments, forcing tools to either work around this opacity or treat virtualised workloads
as black boxes [33].
Software energy attribution is hard for reasons that are not incidental — they are structural. The
hardware provides energy readings at the wrong granularity; its own dynamic behaviour, already
described in Chapter 3, breaks the linear assumptions that simple attribution models rely on; the act
of dividing a shared resource among concurrent entities is ambiguous in ways that no single proxy
metric resolves; and the tools built to perform attribution must operate within an environment
that interferes with, perturbs, and obscures the signal they are trying to capture. Appreciating
the full weight of these challenges is essential for evaluating the trade-offs made by existing tools
and for understanding why finer-grained, more accurate attribution remains an active research
problem. Before turning to those tools, Chapter 5 introduces the system-level interfaces and kernel
abstractions they are built upon.

ENERGY ATTRIBUTION TOOLS AND INTERFACES

The measurement interfaces and hardware mechanisms described in the preceding chapters
establish what the hardware makes observable and at what granularity. What connects those observations to individual software entities is a set of system-level interfaces and abstractions that
attribution tools use to instrument the kernel, query per-process activity, and map kernel-visible
identifiers to higher-level entities such as containers and virtual machines. This chapter introduces
the five principal mechanisms that form this observability infrastructure: Extended Berkeley Packet
Filter (eBPF) as a kernel instrumentation framework enabling fine-grained, event-driven observation; hardware performance counters as a source of per-core activity metrics; the /proc filesystem
as the primary interface for per-process resource usage; Linux control groups as the mechanism
for mapping threads to containers; and Libvirt as the equivalent mapping layer for virtualised
environments.

5.1

eBPF

eBPF is a kernel technology that allows user-space programs to load small, sandboxed programs
into the Linux kernel, where they execute in response to kernel or application events [37]. The name
is a historical artifact — the original Berkeley Packet Filter was a mechanism for filtering network
packets, and eBPF began as an extension of it — but the technology has since been generalised
into a full-purpose kernel instrumentation and observability framework. eBPF is now a standard
component of Linux observability tooling, used in networking, security monitoring, and, increasingly,
energy attribution.

5.1.1

Hook Points

eBPF programs are event-driven: they attach to hook points and execute when the associated
event fires [37]. Pre-defined hook types include tracepoints (stable, versioned instrumentation points
embedded in the kernel source), kprobes (dynamic attachment to arbitrary kernel functions), uprobes (dynamic attachment to user-space functions), system call entry and exit, and network events.
If no suitable pre-defined hook exists, kprobes and uprobes allow attachment at almost any point in
kernel or user-space code. The placement and relationships of these hooks are shown in Figure 5.2,
while the general compilation and verification ecosystem is sketched in Figure 5.1.

5.1.2

Maps

eBPF maps are typed key-value stores residing in kernel memory that serve as the shared data
structure between eBPF programs and user space [37]. Common map types include hash maps for
arbitrary key-value associations, per-CPU arrays for maintaining per-core counters without locking,
ring buffers for efficiently streaming events to user space, and perf event arrays for passing perf event
file descriptors into eBPF programs. Data accumulated by eBPF programs — for instance, Central
Processing Unit (CPU) time per Process Identifier (PID) or cycle counts per scheduling interval —
is written to maps and consumed asynchronously by a user-space agent.

5.1.3

Verifier and Safety

Before any eBPF program executes, the kernel verifier statically analyses it to guarantee that it
cannot crash the kernel, loop infinitely, access arbitrary memory, or otherwise compromise system
integrity [37]. The verifier enforces memory bounds, restricts callable kernel functions, limits stack
size, and requires that all code paths terminate. This safety guarantee is what makes eBPF practical
to deploy in production; it also imposes real design constraints on attribution tools, since certain
patterns — unbounded loops, large stack allocations, or calls to non-whitelisted kernel functions —
are rejected by the verifier regardless of intent.

5.2

Hardware Performance Counters

Modern processors expose a set of programmable hardware counters, managed collectively
by the Performance Monitoring Unit (PMU), that count low-level architectural events on each
core: instructions retired, cache misses, branch mispredictions, memory operations, and many
others. These performance monitoring counters are accessible from user space primarily via the
perf_event_open(2) system call [38], which provides a unified interface to both hardware performance monitoring counters and software event counters maintained by the kernel. Higher-level tools
— perf, PAPI, LIKWID — build on top of this interface and expose counter data to applications
and users.
A practical constraint shapes how performance counters are used in practice. A processor core
has only a small number of physical counter registers — typically four to eight — while the number of
events that can in principle be counted is far larger. When more events are requested than physical

registers allow, the kernel rotates which events are tracked across successive intervals and scales
up the partial counts to estimate what the full-interval total would have been. This introduces
sampling error whose magnitude depends on how rapidly the mix of events changes within each

Two fixed Model-Specific Register counters are worth specific attention in the energy attribution

frequency, while Actual Performance Register (APERF) counts actual cycles at whatever frequency
the core is currently running. Their ratio gives the effective per-core frequency scaling factor at any
given moment, without requiring any query to the operating system or firmware.

5.3

The /proc Filesystem

The /proc filesystem is a virtual filesystem maintained by the Linux kernel that exposes process
and system state as a navigable tree of pseudo-files readable from user space [39]. For energy
attribution tools, three categories of files are relevant:
• Per-process CPU accounting is exposed through /proc/[pid]/stat and /proc/[pid]
/task/[tid]/stat, which report the cumulative CPU time — separately in user mode and
kernel mode — accumulated by a given process or Thread Identifier (TID) since it started
[39]. Differencing successive readings of these files across a polling interval yields the CPU
time consumed by each thread during that window. This is the basis on which polling-based
attribution tools such as Scaphandre compute per-process CPU fractions for proportional
energy partitioning.
• Process identity and hierarchy are available through /proc/[pid]/status and /proc/
[pid]/cmdline, which provide the command name, parent PID, and thread group of a given
process, allowing a tool to associate per-thread measurements with human-readable process
names and to aggregate thread-level measurements to the process level.
• Container and cgroup linkage is exposed through /proc/[pid]/cgroup, which lists
the control group hierarchy memberships of a given process [39]. This is the file through

34

which attribution tools resolve a kernel-visible PID to the container or orchestration entity
it belongs to, as described in Section 5.4.

5.4

Control Groups

Linux control groups (cgroups) are a kernel facility that organises processes into a hierarchical
tree and allows resource limits and accounting to be applied to each node of the hierarchy as a unit
[40]. From an energy attribution perspective, the key fact about cgroups is that container runtimes
use them as their implementation substrate: when Docker, containerd, or any other OCI-compliant
runtime creates a container, it creates a corresponding cgroup and places the container’s processes
into it. Kubernetes pods are similarly mapped to cgroup subtrees, with the cgroup path encoding
the pod’s unique identifier and container name.
This hierarchy makes container-level energy aggregation tractable: a tool that has obtained a
per-thread or per-process energy estimate can read /proc/[pid]/cgroup to determine which cgroup
— and therefore which container or pod — that thread belongs to, and aggregate accordingly. In
Kubernetes environments, the cgroup path typically contains sufficient information to identify the
pod, and the Kubelet Application Programming Interface (API) can be queried to resolve it to full
Kubernetes metadata including namespace, deployment, and labels.

5.5

Libvirt

Libvirt is an open-source virtualisation management library and application programming interface that provides a unified interface to a range of hypervisors including Kernel-based Virtual
Machine (KVM), Xen, and Quick Emulator (QEMU) [41]. It abstracts each Virtual Machine (VM)
as a domain with a stable domain identifier and exposes a consistent programmatic interface regardless of the underlying hypervisor.
From the host operating system’s perspective, a VM running under KVM appears as a QEMU
process — its threads are visible in the host’s process table alongside any other host process. Libvirt
domain identifiers provide the mapping from these host-visible QEMU processes to the virtual
machines they represent. An attribution tool that has computed per-process energy estimates on
the host can use this mapping to aggregate those estimates up to the VM level, enabling perVM energy accounting in exactly the same way that cgroup membership enables per-container
accounting.
The five mechanisms described in this section operate at different layers of the system stack
and serve complementary roles in the attribution pipeline. eBPF provides the instrumentation substrate for event-driven, fine-grained observation at the kernel level. Hardware performance counters,
and the APERF/MPERF pair in particular, supply the per-core activity metrics and effective frequency information that Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling (DVFS)-aware attribution models require to move beyond simple CPU-time proportionality. The /proc filesystem provides the
polling-accessible counterpart: a window into per-process state, exposing CPU time, identity, and
cgroup membership for any running process. Cgroups and Libvirt then serve as the identity layers for those higher-level entities — one for containerised workloads, the other for virtualised ones
— translating process-level measurements into the deployment-relevant abstractions that operators
and schedulers reason about. With this infrastructure established, the following chapter examines
how existing tools and approaches in the literature have built upon it to tackle the attribution
problem in practice.

INTRODUCTION

Energy attribution — the problem of determining how much energy a running software entity
consumes — has become a central concern in modern computing infrastructure. As datacenters
grow in scale and sustainability commitments tighten, the ability to measure, audit, and optimize
energy consumption at the granularity of individual processes and threads is increasingly necessary.
This section reviews the existing literature on fine-grained Central Processing Unit (CPU) energy
attribution in Linux, focusing specifically on CPU and Dynamic Random Access Memory (DRAM)
as the primary attribution targets.
The scope of this review is intentionally bounded. While a full server node draws power across
many subsystems, CPU and DRAM together account for a disproportionate share of node energy
consumption — more than 30% even in Graphics Processing Unit (GPU)-heavy workloads [42, 13].
Beyond raw share, a non-negligible portion of datacenter power is tied directly to CPU activity
[43], and the CPU is the most dynamically complex component in terms of energy behavior: it
operates across a range of voltage and frequency states, enters and exits multiple idle depths within
microseconds, and exhibits fundamentally different power characteristics depending on whether
cores are executing independently or sharing physical resources. These properties make the CPU
both the most important and the most difficult target for fine-grained attribution.
The review covers work that attributes energy based on real, observable system characteristics — Hardware Performance Counter (HwPC), scheduling events, Running Average Power Limit
(RAPL) readings, and memory footprint — rather than work oriented toward stable worst-case or
game-theoretic allocations. Within this scope, the reviewed literature divides naturally into two
attribution goals: approaches that aim for a consistent, reproducible value per entity independent
of co-running workloads, and approaches that aim to reflect what is actually happening on the live
system. The remainder of this part proceeds as follows: Chapter 7 characterizes both attribution
goals and identifies the one that motivates the bulk of the reviewed work. Chapter 8 surveys the
key research within that goal, organized by approach type. Chapter 9 synthesizes the findings in a
comparative table, and Chapter 10 and the final chapter identify the central open gap and conclude
the thesis.

ENERGY ATTRIBUTION GOALS

A foundational distinction runs through the reviewed literature, one that Cadorel and Saingre
[44] make explicit through their formal treatment of power division: attribution methods differ not
only in their technical mechanisms but in what they are fundamentally trying to achieve. Using the
conceptual framework that paper introduces, the reviewed works can be organized into two camps.
Some produce an attribution that is stable — consistent and reproducible regardless of what else is
co-running on the same machine. Others produce an attribution that is context-dependent — one
that shifts as the surrounding system state changes, and deliberately so. The first goal is examined
briefly in Section 7.1; the second is the framing adopted by the majority of the reviewed literature
and is the focus of the rest of this chapter.

7.1

Stable Attribution

A stable attribution method assigns a consistent energy value to a software entity — one that
does not fluctuate as co-runners appear or disappear on the same machine. This property is meaningful for software energy optimization, where a developer comparing two versions of the same code
needs confidence that differences in attribution reflect differences in the code rather than differences
in the surrounding execution context. Two examples from the reviewed literature realize this goal
in different ways: an isolation-based worst-case estimation approach and a game-theoretic fair-share
method.
CINERGY [45] approaches stability by stepping back from the live system entirely. Rather than
using fine-grained hardware metrics as a proxy for process-level work, it uses a more abstract input
— Central Processing Unit (CPU) usage — to build a polynomial regression model that estimates
the Worst-Case Power Consumption (WCPC) a Virtual Machine (VM) would draw running in
isolation, without any energy-efficiency gains from workload consolidation. The resulting estimate
is stable by construction: because it ignores the system context entirely, it cannot be perturbed by
changes in the surrounding execution environment.
Shapley-based methods approach stable attribution from a different direction, drawing on Cooperative Game Theory to define what a fair attribution should look like across all possible co-runner
combinations. In the Shapley framing, each process is a player in a cooperative game, and the total
energy consumed during a time interval is the value to be divided. A coalition is any subset of the
active processes that could run together, and the value of a coalition is the energy the system would
consume if only its members were running. The marginal contribution of a player to a coalition
is the additional energy consumed by the system when that player joins — that is, the difference
between the coalition’s energy with the player and without it. The Shapley value then assigns each
player a share equal to its average marginal contribution, averaged over all possible orderings in

This value is uniquely characterized by four fairness axioms it simultaneously satisfies: efficiency
(all attributions sum to the total measured energy), symmetry (two processes with identical marginal
contributions in every coalition receive equal attribution), the null player property (a process that
adds no energy in any coalition receives zero), and additivity (attribution across two consecutive
intervals equals attribution over their union). While the primary objective here is optimization, these
axioms also position the Shapley value as a theoretically principled basis for tenant billing, since
they guarantee that charges are consistent, reflect actual contribution, and cannot be manipulated
by changing the order of attribution. The core practical obstacle is that computing the Shapley
value exactly requires knowing the energy of every possible coalition, and the number of coalitions
grows exponentially with the number of processes — a set of n processes yields 2n coalitions. The
two reviewed works each propose a different strategy to address this.
Dong et al. [46] apply the Shapley method to attribute energy to applications in a mobile system,
where the goal is to identify which application is the top energy consumer and to support energy
management decisions. The authors tackle the 2n tractability problem through three complementary
techniques. First, they shorten the measurement interval to as little as 10 ms: in a sufficiently short
window, fewer processes are simultaneously active, which directly reduces the number of distinct
coalitions that need to be evaluated. Second, since not all coalitions will be observed in any finite
run, missing coalition values are estimated through a recursive bootstrapping procedure — initial
Shapley shares are used to fill in unobserved coalition energies, the resulting complete table is used
to recompute shares, and the procedure iterates until convergence. Third, they introduce M-games,
a reduced game formulation in which only coalitions up to a maximum size m are considered,
rather than all 2n subsets; this limits the number of required measurements to a tractable set
while still satisfying the Shapley axioms on the restricted game. The combined result is a practical
approximation of the Shapley value that can be computed from measurements collected passively
as the system runs.
Jiang et al. [47] apply the Shapley framework to VM-level power attribution on a physical
server, targeting the problem of fair tenant billing in virtualized datacenters. The exponential
coalition space is made tractable through Virtual Homogeneous Coalition: rather than treating each
individual VM as a distinct player, VMs of the same configuration type — defined by their vCPU
count and memory allocation — are grouped together, and the game is played over types rather
than instances. In a typical datacenter where VMs come in a small number of fixed configuration
templates (e.g., at most five instance types in Amazon EC2), this reduces the coalition space from 2n
individual VMs to 2r type combinations, where r is the number of distinct types. For each coalition
of types, power is approximated using a linear model fitted to measurements collected during an
offline campaign with a physical external power meter; the Shapley calculation is then performed on
these approximated values at runtime at 1 Hz. The fairness axioms directly support the billing use
case: since the Shapley value guarantees efficiency and symmetry, tenants with identical workloads
are charged equally, and the sum of all charges equals the measured power of the physical machine.
These two examples illustrate that stable attribution is achievable, but through mechanisms that
are deliberately decoupled from the live system state. The rest of this review turns to the larger
body of work that takes the opposite approach — treating context-dependence not as a problem to
be eliminated, but as the defining feature of a useful attribution.

7.2

Context-Dependent Attribution

Where stable attribution deliberately abstracts away the transient state of the system, contextdependent attribution takes the opposite stance: the attributed energy of a process or container
should reflect what the hardware is actually doing at the moment of measurement. Power consumption on a modern server is not static — it varies continuously with workload intensity, CPU
frequency, idle state transitions, and interference from co-running tenants. A context-dependent
method tracks all of this, producing attribution values that change as the system state changes.
This goal is motivated by several concrete use cases. Energy-aware process and container
scheduling requires that a scheduler can observe, at runtime, how much energy each running process
is actually drawing, so that placement and migration decisions can be informed by real consumption rather than estimates [48]. A concrete illustration of this need is EC6 [49], which improves
energy efficiency in Kubernetes by dynamically migrating idle containers to a reserved core so that
the remaining cores can sustain deep C-state residency — a scheduling decision that presupposes
knowledge of which containers are drawing power and why. Energy-based billing similarly requires
that a tenant’s attributed share of node energy reflects their actual resource consumption over a
billing period, not a conservative envelope [44].
All work reviewed from this point onward pursues context-dependent attribution. Within this
goal, the reviewed literature divides into two methodological families. Model-based approaches
learn the relationship between hardware signals and power consumption from training data, and
use the resulting model to estimate and distribute energy at runtime. Formula-based approaches
instead derive attribution analytically from hardware semantics, applying fixed formulas grounded
in documented hardware behavior. The following chapter examines each family in turn.

All work reviewed from this point targets context-dependent attribution — attribution that
reflects the live state of the system, shifting as hardware and workload conditions change. Within
this goal, the reviewed literature divides along a further axis: whether attribution is driven by a
learned model or by an analytical formula derived from hardware semantics. This section covers
both in turn.

8.1

Model-Based Approaches

Approximately 11 of the reviewed works use a model-based approach, compared to roughly 8
that rely on a formula-based one — making model-based methods the plurality in the literature.
This prevalence reflects the inherent flexibility of learned models: rather than requiring the developer to analytically enumerate every hardware effect, a model can learn complex, non-linear
relationships between observable signals and actual power consumption from empirical data. Hardware Performance Counter (HwPC)s are the dominant input choice across these works, directly
reflecting what is happening on the processor — instruction throughput, cache pressure, memory
access patterns, branch behavior — offering a far more informative view of hardware activity than
coarse Operating System (OS)-level metrics such as Central Processing Unit (CPU) utilization [50].
Looking across this body of work, three recurring limitations can be identified: whether a model
trained on one hardware configuration transfers to another (hardware generalizability); whether
the deployment mechanism is practical at fleet scale (deployability); and whether the model degrades silently when production workloads shift away from those seen at training time (workload
generalizability).
A first group of works either overlooks these limitations or addresses them only through full
retraining from scratch. Bader et al. [51] train an `1 -regularized regression model correlating perprocess Extended Berkeley Packet Filter (eBPF) metrics against an external smart meter, trained
and evaluated on a single node with no cross-system validation and no transfer mechanism. The
iteration of Kepler detailed in Amaral et al. [52] trains frequency-range regression models normalized against real-time Running Average Power Limit (RAPL) readings for bare-metal nodes,
but distributes pre-trained models — fitted on a specific Intel Xeon E5-2667 v3 — for deployment
environments where hardware power access is unavailable, and applies them to arbitrary target
hardware without retraining [53]. BitWatts [54] builds per-core polynomial regression models per
CPU frequency range using two fixed HwPC — unhalted cycles and reference cycles — against an
external PowerSpy meter, requiring a full learning phase per node. WattsApp [55] trains a separate neural network per container over resource utilization statistics using a WattsUp USB meter
as the oracle, with no cross-system validation and no retraining automation — meaning the per-

for handling new hardware is either absent or requires a full manual retraining campaign, making
large-scale fleet deployment impractical.
WattsKit [56] represents a meaningful step forward on both hardware generalizability and
deployability. Rather than fixing a set of HwPC in advance, WattsKit selects them dynamically
through a structured learning phase: representative benchmarks are executed on the target node, all
available HwPC candidates are sampled briefly, and those whose Pearson correlation with measured
power falls below a threshold are discarded before any full-length training run takes place [56]. Full
training runs are then performed using only the retained counters, with the final model built from
the hardest-to-model benchmarks on the rationale that generalization on difficult cases implies
generalization on easier ones. The counter combination yielding the lowest regression error on heldout benchmarks is selected. Because counter selection is determined entirely by measured correlation
on the target machine, the approach makes no prior assumptions about CPU architecture, and the
model structure explicitly accounts for Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling (DVFS), C-states,
Simultaneous Multithreading (SMT), and TurboBoost. WattsKit achieves an average error of 1.35%
(1.60 W) on an Intel Xeon W3520.

On hardware generalizability and deployability, SmartWatts is a genuine advance: no offline
calibration phase is required, HwPC selection adapts to whatever events the host machine exposes,
and the tool can be deployed on any machine with RAPL support and Linux perf events without

43

node-specific configuration. The remaining limitation is structural. HwPC selection is fixed at the
end of the initial event selection phase, which runs for a configurable window of 30 ticks on whatever
workload happens to be active at sensor startup. If the production workload subsequently shifts in
type, the elected counters may no longer be the most informative ones, and the model will continue
using a selection optimized for a context that no longer holds.
SelfWatts [58] builds directly on SmartWatts’ frequency-specific modeling architecture and
targets precisely this remaining limitation. Where SmartWatts fixes its HwPC selection after an
initial calibration window, SelfWatts replaces this static selection with a continuous, feedback-driven
Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE) loop that revises the active counter set at runtime based on
observed model accuracy — with no offline calibration phase at any point.
The architecture of SelfWatts, shown in Figure 8.4, distributes its components across the infrastructure: a Sensor and Controller pair runs on each monitored host, while the Formula component
— responsible for model inference and power estimation — can run either co-located on the same
host or remotely in the PowerAPI backend alongside a Time-Series Database (TSDB), as illustrated
in Figure 8.5. The Sensor collects RAPL readings and HwPC samples per cgroup; the Controller,
using the Libpfm4 library to enumerate all Performance Monitoring Unit events available on the
host, configures which events the Sensor monitors. The Formula builds frequency-specific power
models from these samples using Lasso regression and the APERF/MPERF MSR ratio — exactly
as in SmartWatts — and continuously tracks model accuracy via a sliding window median error.
When accuracy degrades beyond a threshold, the Formula applies RFE, a method that iteratively
removes the least predictive input features from the model, to identify which currently monitored
events are irrelevant. The Controller then replaces those events with unexplored candidates while
retaining all events the active model already relies on. The behavioral consequence is the important
one: the counter set the model depends on is never dropped; only irrelevant counters cycle through
new candidates, allowing the model to converge toward a better selection as the workload type
changes, without human intervention.
SelfWatts is validated across three distinct Intel micro-architectures — Skylake, Broadwell, and
Haswell — demonstrating that the zero-configuration approach converges to an accurate model
regardless of target hardware. Two limitations are worth noting. The online exploration phase
produces transient estimation errors that exceed those of a stable SmartWatts instance; for use cases
demanding continuous attribution reliability — such as energy billing — this transient degradation
is a meaningful concern. Additionally, the distributed deployment configuration, where the Formula
runs on a remote node to reduce overhead on the monitored machine, is not experimentally validated
in the paper.
No reviewed model-based work resolves all three limitations without trade-offs: hardware generalizability, deployability, and workload generalizability each pull in different directions, and the
design choices made to address one tend to introduce costs elsewhere — whether in the form of
additional infrastructure requirements, convergence periods, or residual calibration dependencies.
Formula-based approaches, examined in the following section, sidestep the counter-selection problem
entirely — but impose a different kind of constraint that will become apparent from the reviewed
works.

8.2

Formula-Based Approaches

Formula-based approaches replace learned models with fixed analytical formulas derived from
hardware semantics. Rather than fitting a model to training data, these tools apply formulas
grounded in documented hardware behavior — most commonly distributing RAPL readings proportionally according to some per-process proxy for hardware activity. This design choice brings an
important operational advantage: there is no training phase, no counter-selection procedure, and
no offline calibration requirement, making these tools straightforward to deploy across heterogeneous fleets. The cost of this simplicity is an equally hard requirement: every source of hardware

44

Figure 8.4: SelfWatts four-layer architecture
complexity that affects energy consumption must be explicitly and correctly accounted for in the
formula. Any hardware effect absent from the formula becomes a systematic attribution error.
The most widely deployed tools in this category share a common attribution logic: each process’s
share of total active CPU time is read from /proc, multiplied by the RAPL socket energy delta for
that interval, and assigned as the process’s energy for that period. Scaphandre [59] is a lightweight
daemon that applies this logic directly against RAPL via the powercap interface. Kepler [53]
extends the same model to support attribution at the process, container, virtual machine, and
pod levels, integrating natively with Kubernetes cluster monitoring. KubeWatt [60] introduces a
notable refinement: rather than distributing static power among running containers, it treats it
as unattributed cluster overhead, and distributes dynamic power proportionally by container CPU
utilization share computed relative to the sum across containers rather than against the node total,
avoiding double-counting of system-level processes. PowerJoular [61] also relies on a proportional
utilization approach but uses CPU cycles rather than time as its proxy.
The shared limitation of this group runs deeper than individual implementation choices. CPU
time and CPU utilization are frequency-blind proxies [62]: two processes occupying equal fractions
of CPU time are assigned equal energy shares regardless of the voltage-frequency operating point
at which each is executing. More broadly, this class of attribution is blind to SMT co-execution
effects, Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA) topology, C-state transitions on idle cores, and
dynamic performance boost states — all of which cause the actual power drawn by the socket to
diverge from what a time-proportional model would predict.
DEEP-Mon [63] targets fine-grained, per-thread power attribution for containerized workloads
running on a Linux system, and addresses the SMT limitation that the proportional tools above
ignore entirely. Building on the observation that there is a 0.99 linear correlation between Unhalted
Core Cycles (UCC) and CPU power consumption [64], DEEP-Mon replaces CPU time with UCC as
the attribution proxy. To handle SMT, it separates the cycles a thread accumulates while executing

45

Figure 8.5: SelfWatts deployment with Controller and Sensor
alone on a physical core from those accumulated during concurrent execution with another thread
via HyperThreading (HT). For solo execution phases, cycles are counted at face value. For coexecution phases, the empirically measured power increase when a physical core is shared between
two threads is accounted for by scaling those cycles by a measured coefficient and dividing by two,
so that each of the two co-running threads carries an equal share of the overhead — yielding a
weighted cycle count that more accurately reflects the thread’s actual energy contribution. RAPL
package energy is then distributed proportionally by weighted cycle share across all threads. These
per-thread cycle counts are accumulated in kernel space via eBPF hooks on sched_switch, with the
aggregation window — the interval over which cycles are collected before RAPL is read — adjusted
dynamically based on context-switch frequency to avoid attributing a thread’s work to the wrong
measurement period. The resulting per-thread attribution is then mapped to containers via cgroup
identifiers. As illustrated in Figure 8.6, the eBPF program shares data with the user-space agent
through a thread map, a processor topology map for HT sibling tracking, and a configuration map;
the agent’s runtime manager selects the window length and forwards samples to the monitoring
backend.

Figure 8.6: DEEP-Mon monitoring agent structure
DEEP-Mon explicitly models SMT, which the proportional tools do not, but operates without
any frequency correction — cycles are counted regardless of the operating frequency at which they
were executed. NUMA topology is not considered, DRAM energy is not attributed, and the tool
was validated only on dual-socket servers due to an eBPF stack size constraint at the time.
EnergAt [33] approaches the attribution problem differently, aiming for thread-level, NUMAaware energy attribution in multi-tenant environments. Its core observation is that aggregating
resource usage across all sockets before distributing energy is incorrect in multi-socket NUMA
systems, because each socket’s RAPL counter is independent and each socket may be operating at a
different utilization level and power draw. EnergAt addresses this by estimating a per-thread socket

46

residence probability — the fraction of time each thread was scheduled on each socket — derived
by sampling the thread’s scheduling state multiple times within each measurement interval and
averaging those observations to approximate the continuous residence fraction. This is combined
with the thread’s total CPU time to yield a per-socket CPU time estimate, which is used as the
credit coefficient for that socket’s dynamic RAPL energy. Static power is subtracted from the RAPL
total before attribution and distributed separately. For DRAM, the amount of private memory a
process has allocated — that is, the memory pages it exclusively owns, excluding memory shared
with other processes — per NUMA node serves as the credit coefficient for the corresponding DRAM
domain’s dynamic energy. Both the CPU and DRAM credit coefficients are raised to empirically
calibrated, machine-specific exponents to account for the non-linear relationship between utilization
and power.

Figure 8.7: EnergAt energy credit during parallel execution
EnergAt’s model considers NUMA and multi-socket topology, which DEEP-Mon ignores entirely, and separately attributes both CPU and DRAM energy. It does not, however, model SMT
co-execution or apply any frequency correction, and shared memory is excluded from DRAM attribution.
METRION [27] represents the most complete coverage of hardware effects among the reviewed formula-based tools, jointly addressing DVFS, SMT, and NUMA within a single attribution
model. Rather than treating an entire polling interval as a uniform block, METRION operates at
the granularity of individual execution intervals — the time between a thread’s scheduling-in and
scheduling-out events, captured via an eBPF hook on sched_switch. Each interval is further split
into sub-intervals depending on whether the thread was executing alone on its physical core or in
SMT co-execution with another thread, a distinction resolved in post-processing by examining the
recorded scheduling timestamps of all threads on the same physical core and identifying overlapping execution intervals. For CPU attribution, UCC is scaled by the APERF/MPERF frequency
ratio to account for DVFS effects, including Turbo Boost, which is absorbed implicitly by this realtime frequency measurement rather than modeled as a separate case. Co-execution sub-intervals
receive an additional SMT scaling factor, matching the empirical correction used by DEEP-Mon.
For DRAM attribution, Last Level Cache (LLC)-miss loads per DRAM component are tracked and
remote accesses are weighted to reflect the higher energy cost of cross-NUMA memory transactions.
As illustrated in Figure 8.8, this decomposition allows METRION to apply the correct frequency
and SMT correction at each sub-interval rather than averaging over a coarser window. METRION
achieves a Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) of 4.2% for CPU-bound workloads and 16.1%
for memory-bound workloads, at a runtime overhead of 21.1% — the highest among the tools evaluated, attributed to the Python implementation. For context, across the same workloads Scaphandre
achieves an average MAPE of 32.4% and EnergAt 69.3% for CPU attribution, with runtime overheads of 9.2% and 6.5% respectively [27]. The results across workload types are shown in Figure 8.9.

A common thread runs through the reviewed literature regardless of approach family: the
correctness of any attribution method depends on how accurately it accounts for the hardware
mechanisms that govern energy consumption at the level of individual cores and memory domains.
For model-based approaches, this dependency is indirect but no less real. A learned model has the
capacity to capture complex, non-linear hardware behaviour implicitly, but this capacity comes with
a fundamental risk: a model that captures the energy dynamics of one context well may silently
degrade in another, with no signal that this has occurred. The absence of deliberate consideration of
a hardware effect — no explicit architectural provision, no targeted validation — leaves correctness
unverifiable. For formula-based approaches, the dependency is direct and unforgiving: a hardware
effect that is not explicitly modelled is simply absent from the attribution, regardless of how accurate
the formula is in all other respects.
A further observation concerns the range of strategies the model-based literature has explored
in response to the context overfitting problem. These strategies form a rough spectrum from static
to fully adaptive, from counter selection fixed at calibration time to continuous online revision via
recursive feature elimination. Moving along this spectrum toward greater adaptability does reduce
dependence on pre-deployment assumptions, but each step introduces its own costs — deployment
constraints, convergence periods before stable attribution, or continuous computational overhead
during production. No point on this spectrum is clearly dominant: greater adaptability buys
portability at the cost of stability and overhead, while static approaches offer predictable behaviour
at the cost of brittleness when conditions diverge from training.
Formula-based approaches face a related but structurally distinct generalizability problem.
Where model-based approaches risk overfitting to a training context, formula-based approaches
embed their hardware assumptions directly as fixed empirical constants — power ratios for SMT
co-execution, scaling exponents for non-linear CPU utilization, penalty factors for remote NUMA
memory access. These constants are measured on specific hardware and applied universally, with no
mechanism to detect when the deployment context makes them inappropriate. A tool may therefore correctly account for a hardware effect in principle while remaining numerically inaccurate
on hardware it was not calibrated for. Resolving this portability problem, without sacrificing the
transparency and deployability that make formula-based approaches attractive, remains an open
challenge.
Looking across both families, the reviewed works collectively demonstrate that fine-grained
energy attribution is achievable and that meaningful progress has been made on the hardware
effects that complicate it. The two families, however, face structurally different challenges. Modelbased approaches struggle with the conditions under which their accuracy holds — deployability
and generalizability remain in tension with one another regardless of how adaptive the counter
selection strategy becomes. Formula-based approaches struggle with completeness and portability
— hardware effects that are not explicitly modelled are simply absent, and the fixed constants that
encode what is modelled do not transfer freely across platforms. What Table 9.1 makes visible is
that no existing approach resolves both dimensions simultaneously, and that this gap motivates the
discussion that follows.

CHAPTER 10
DISCUSSION AND GAP IDENTIFICATION

The reviewed literature reveals that no existing approach to fine-grained Central Processing
Unit (CPU) and Dynamic Random Access Memory (DRAM) energy attribution satisfies the full
set of requirements that make such a tool practically useful. The problem is not a lack of effort
in any single direction, but a combination of unresolved trade-offs and structural limitations that
persist across both model-based and formula-based families.
Model-based approaches, at their most capable, offer adaptability to hardware diversity and
the ability to capture non-linear power behavior. SmartWatts [57] and SelfWatts [58] represent
the furthest progress in this direction, with the latter eliminating any offline calibration requirement through continuous online counter selection. However, the overhead and transient accuracy
degradation introduced by these mechanisms remain barriers to production deployment at scale.
The core tension in model-based approaches — between generalizability, accuracy, and overhead —
has not been fully resolved. A promising direction toward addressing the generalizability side of
this tension is illustrated by PPEM-BM [65], which trains individual power models on monitored
servers, then uses a Learning to Rank model to select the most hardware-similar pre-trained model
for any new unmonitored node — eliminating the need for per-node retraining. The approach is
originally motivated by server-level power estimation in bare-metal environments, but the principle
it demonstrates is relevant more broadly: model portability through hardware-similarity ranking
rather than online learning. As shown in Figure 10.1, the ranking model does not always select the
optimal source model, introducing an inherent accuracy trade-off, but the direction is concrete.
Beyond the hardware coverage and generalizability problems, the reviewed approaches share
a second class of limitations in how they handle energy components that exist independently of
any individual process’s work. Cadorel and Saingre [44] identify three conceptually distinct energy
components: active energy, which reflects the marginal cost of a process’s computational work; idle
energy, which the machine draws regardless of what processes are running; and residual energy,
which arises from the act of having at least one core active and is correlated with the execution
context rather than any single process’s behavior, making it impossible to cleanly attribute to any
one process. While idle power has several established measurement approaches in the literature,
residual energy is harder to isolate, and the only concrete measurement method proposed in the
reviewed work is that of Kepler [52]: run two identical workloads in sequence, starting the second
after the first is already running, and record the power increment at the moment the second workload
launches; the difference between the total single-instance power and this increment isolates the
residual component.
The Greenhouse Gas Protocol [66], as invoked in Kepler [52], provides a principled basis for
treating both idle and residual energy as shared infrastructure costs that should be accounted for
separately rather than absorbed into per-process active attribution. Most reviewed tools do not
follow this separation: idle energy is distributed proportionally among running processes despite

being entirely independent of their presence, and residual energy is silently lumped into active
shares. The approach taken by KubeWatt [60], which treats idle power as an unattributed system
overhead rather than dividing it among containers, is the closest alignment with this principle
in the reviewed literature, and illustrates that the separation is both feasible and architecturally
meaningful.
A further and more fundamental flaw in the reviewed approaches is identified by Bellal et al.
[36], who challenge not a specific tool but the attribution mechanism that underlies virtually all
reviewed solutions. The shared mechanism, across both families, is to compute a per-process weight
— from CPU time, model output, hardware counter readings, or a combination — and distribute
total energy proportionally based on that weight. The structural problem is that this proportional
distribution shares any change in socket-level power across all running processes, regardless of which
core caused it.
The most concrete failure mode involves C-states. When idle cores enter deep sleep states, the
socket’s total power drops. A proportional model redistributes this saving across all processes, including those running on fully active cores whose workload has not changed at all. As illustrated in
Figure 10.2, a workload running on a dedicated active core with a constant load sees its attributed
power drop significantly when C-states are enabled on other cores — not because its actual consumption changed, but because the socket total fell and the proportional share was recalculated
accordingly.
This is not a calibration failure. It is a structural consequence of proportional distribution
against a socket-level total, which has no mechanism for distinguishing energy changes that originate on idle cores from those that originate on active ones. Even METRION [27], the most
hardware-aware formula-based tool reviewed, does not escape this limitation. METRION weights
each thread’s contribution by its effective frequency ratio — meaning a core in a deep idle state,
whose effective frequency approaches zero, absorbs almost none of the socket energy. This is conceptually the right response to C-states, and it mitigates the problem. But because METRION
still distributes the socket-level total proportionally across cores, the energy savings attributable to
a specific idle core cannot be precisely isolated, and the structural issue identified by Bellal et al.
remains present, if partially attenuated.
The gap that emerges is therefore threefold. First, no reviewed tool achieves joint real-time
coverage of all relevant hardware mechanisms — Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling (DVFS),
Simultaneous Multithreading (SMT), Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA), C-states, and dy-

CONCLUSION

The reviewed literature reveals that fine-grained Central Processing Unit (CPU) and Dynamic
Random Access Memory (DRAM) energy attribution has converged around two broad methodological families — model-based and formula-based — each of which has made genuine progress
while running into a structurally distinct ceiling. Model-based approaches have grown increasingly
capable of adapting to hardware diversity and shifting workload conditions, with the most advanced
tools eliminating offline calibration entirely through continuous online counter selection. Formulabased approaches have progressively extended their coverage of hardware effects, reaching a point
where Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling (DVFS), Simultaneous Multithreading (SMT), and
Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA) topology can each be handled within a single analytical
framework. Yet neither family has resolved the full problem.
The limitations that remain are not incidental. Three persistent gaps emerge from the synthesis:
• No reviewed tool achieves joint, real-time coverage of all relevant hardware mechanisms without either excessive overhead or a post-processing requirement that precludes live attribution.
• Idle and residual energy are not consistently treated as separate components, and most tools
absorb them silently into per-process active shares — a misattribution that is independent
of, and compounds with, the accuracy of the attribution formula itself.
• Most fundamentally, the proportional distribution mechanism that underlies virtually all
reviewed approaches — across both families — is structurally incapable of correctly isolating
energy changes that originate on idle cores from those that originate on active ones [36]. This
is not a calibration problem that better training data or more expressive formulas can fix; it
is a consequence of distributing a socket-level total without per-core visibility.
These three dimensions together define the open problem that the reviewed literature has not
closed: per-process CPU and DRAM energy attribution that correctly models energy behavior
at the core level, treats the non-active components of node power as separate, and does so with
overhead compatible with production deployment.

Final Remarks

57

CONCLUSION

This thesis has provided a comprehensive overview of the scientific and technical landscape
underlying fine-grained Central Processing Unit (CPU) and Dynamic Random Access Memory
(DRAM) energy attribution in Linux systems, examining the measurement interfaces, hardware
mechanisms, and attribution approaches that define the current state of the field.
The analysis reveals that genuine and meaningful progress has been made. Model-based approaches have advanced from static, node-specific training pipelines toward fully adaptive, selfcalibrating systems capable of operating across diverse hardware without offline calibration. Formulabased approaches have progressively extended their coverage of hardware effects, reaching a point
where Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling (DVFS), Simultaneous Multithreading (SMT), and
Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA) topology can each be addressed within a single analytical
framework. These are real achievements, and they reflect a maturing understanding of the problem.
Yet the synthesis also reveals that no existing approach resolves the full problem. Three structural limitations persist across both methodological families. First, no reviewed tool achieves joint,
real-time coverage of all relevant hardware mechanisms without either excessive overhead or a postprocessing requirement that precludes live attribution. Second, idle and residual energy are not
consistently separated from active attribution, meaning a portion of node power that is independent of any individual process’s behaviour is routinely and silently misassigned. Third, and most
fundamentally, the proportional distribution mechanism that underlies virtually all reviewed approaches cannot correctly isolate energy changes at the granularity at which they originate — a
consequence of distributing a socket-level total without per-core visibility, rather than a limitation
that more accurate formulas or better-trained models can overcome.
These gaps are not incidental. They follow directly from the hardware complexity surveyed in
this work: a landscape in which energy is measured at the wrong granularity, in which dynamic
behaviour continuously undermines the assumptions that simple attribution models rely on, and
in which dividing a shared resource among concurrent consumers is structurally ambiguous. This
analysis establishes a precise formulation of the open problem — identifying the conditions a correct
attribution method would need to satisfy and the reasons existing tools fall short — and establishes
a clear basis for deciding where and how existing tools can be meaningfully improved.
